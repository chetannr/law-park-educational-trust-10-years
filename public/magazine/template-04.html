To Every Child, Every Donor,
Every Well-Wisher

"Education is not a gift we give to children. It is a right that belongs to them. Our only job is to make sure nothing stands in the way."

Ten years ago, this journey began with a simple belief: every child, no matter where they are born or how much money their family has, deserves a chance to learn.

We started small — one child, one scholarship, one village visit. We drove to Chickaballapur, sat with children and their parents under open skies, and listened to stories of dreams that just needed a little help to come true.

Today, as we mark a decade of this work, we are filled with gratitude. Gratitude for the children who trusted us. Gratitude for the parents who believed us. Gratitude for every donor, volunteer, and partner who said: "Yes, let's do this together."

This magazine is our way of saying thank you — and of reminding ourselves why we started.

We hope these pages make you smile, make you proud, and inspire you to walk a little further with us.

Founder & Managing Trustee

Charulatha

10 Years of Believing in Every Child

Across India's villages and small towns, millions of children want to go to school. They are bright, curious, and ready to learn. But somewhere between that dream and the classroom, money gets in the way. Families are forced to make impossible choices. Quietly, one by one, children drop out — not because they don't want to study, but because nobody stepped in at the right moment.

Law Park Educational Trust was born to be that step. We are a registered educational trust based in HSR Layout, Bengaluru. Since 2016, we have been travelling to rural areas across India — from Chickaballapur to MM Hills tribal villages — to find children who need partial scholarship support to stay in school.

Our first school visit — Chickaballapur, 2016
Education opens doors

Our Four-Step Process

1

Identify

School principals, well-wishers, and community members nominate deserving students from income-deprived backgrounds who need support to continue their education.

2

Validate

We visit the location, gather students and parents, and interview each family to verify their situation — with care, respect, and no shortcuts.

3

Embrace

We cover up to 75% of a child's tuition fees at their current school. Parents contribute the remaining part — because involvement creates ownership. Anything given fully free has no lasting value.

4

Incubate

Fees are paid directly to the school — not through parents — so children can focus solely on their studies and tap their fullest potential.

Did you know?

We pay school fees directly to schools — not through parents — so every rupee is accountable and every child benefits.

"I Almost Stopped Going to School"

Meena's Story

Meena was twelve when her father lost his daily wage work for two months. The school sent home a reminder about unpaid fees. She sat outside her house, wondering if she would have to stop studying.

Her teacher knew about Law Park Educational Trust. A few weeks later, a team visited her village, met her family, and asked Meena what she wanted to become.

"A teacher," she said quietly. Her fees were taken care of. She is now in the 9th standard and attended a career counselling session organised by the Trust. She knows exactly which stream she wants to take after 10th.

"The School Bag Changed Everything"

Raju's Story

Raju lives near MM Hills. Until last year, he carried his books in a torn plastic bag. He was embarrassed to walk into school. When the Trust visited with 300 school bags, Raju got a new one. For a ten-year-old boy, that bag meant he belonged — just like every other child. His mother says he now wakes up early every morning, packs his bag the night before, and never misses a day of school.

"A Library in My School"

Sunita's Story

Sunita had never read a storybook. Her school had no library — just textbooks and chalk. When Law Park set up a small library, she picked up a story about a girl who becomes a doctor. She read it three times. Today she visits the library every week. She wants to be a doctor — because she read about one in a book that someone donated.

A donated book is not just paper and ink. It is someone's dream, passed on to a child who needed it.
